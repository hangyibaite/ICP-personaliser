# Ambiguity Handling

How to follow up and narrow down when someone gives a vague, thin, confused, or contradictory answer. This file is the playbook for every moment where the person does not know what they are saying or cannot articulate what they mean.

---

## The Core Principle

People are not being difficult when they give vague answers. They genuinely do not know. Your job is not to punish them for it — it is to give them a different angle of approach until something clicks. Every technique below is a different door into the same room.

---

## Technique 1: The Mirror Flip

**When to use:** They cannot describe their ideal client at all. They say "I don't know" or give you a useless generality.

**How it works:**
1. Stop asking about the client. Ask the same question about them personally.
2. Get a detailed answer about themselves.
3. Extrapolate: "Would your ideal client share this? Is this the kind of person you'd want to work with?"

**Why it works:** Personal-brand founders almost always attract people who mirror themselves. They know themselves. They don't know their avatar yet. Use what they know.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "I don't know what my ideal client does on a Saturday night."
>
> You: "Let's flip it. What do YOU do on a Saturday night? Not what you think you should say — what actually happens."
>
> Them: "Honestly I'm probably watching YouTube, maybe some Hormozi stuff or a podcast, or I'm at the gym earlier and then just chilling."
>
> You: "So your ideal client — same vibe? Someone who's consuming business content for fun, values fitness, not out partying? Would you want to work with someone who spends their weekends like you do?"

---

## Technique 2: The Concrete Incident

**When to use:** They give you an adjective instead of a behavior. "My ideal client is driven." "They should be motivated." "I want someone coachable."

**How it works:**
1. Reject the adjective. Name it as unusable.
2. Ask for a specific real incident that demonstrates the trait.
3. Extract the behavior from the incident.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "I want clients who are driven."
>
> You: "Driven is an adjective, not a filter — everyone thinks they're driven. Tell me about a specific moment with a client where you thought 'this person gets it.' What did they actually do or say?"
>
> Them: "There was this one guy who messaged me at like 2am because he'd already tried implementing what I said in the last call and had follow-up questions. He didn't wait for the next session."
>
> You: "So 'driven' for you means: implements immediately, doesn't wait to be told, comes back with specific follow-up questions rather than 'what do I do next.' Is that right?"

---

## Technique 3: The Worst Client Inversion

**When to use:** They cannot describe who they want. But almost everyone can describe who they hate.

**How it works:**
1. Ask about their worst client experience.
2. Let them vent. Get the full picture.
3. Invert every trait to define what they actually want.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "I honestly don't know what personality I'm looking for."
>
> You: "Different angle — tell me about the worst client you've ever had. Or the worst prospect interaction. What made it awful?"
>
> Them: "This one person took forever to reply, like 4-5 days between messages. When they did reply it was one word. They paid and then expected me to just do everything. Never did the work I assigned."
>
> You: "So inverting that — your ideal client responds fast, communicates in full thoughts, treats the engagement as a partnership where they do the work alongside you, and doesn't treat payment as a delegation of effort. Right?"

---

## Technique 4: The Narrowing Reframe

**When to use:** They give you a range so wide it's useless. "My clients are 18-50." "I serve anyone in business." "Any industry works."

**How it works:**
1. Name the range as too wide to be actionable.
2. Force a single-point answer instead of a range.
3. Then allow them to expand slightly from that anchor.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "My age range is 18-45."
>
> You: "An 18-year-old living with their parents and a 45-year-old with a mortgage and kids are completely different people. Your copy can't speak to both at the same time. So: picture the one person who sees your content and immediately thinks 'this is for me.' How old are they? One number."
>
> Them: "Probably like 27."
>
> You: "Good. So the avatar is 27. People aged 22-32 probably still resonate, but 27 is who you write to. That's the anchor."

---

## Technique 5: The Behavioral Translation

**When to use:** They give you a values-based answer that cannot be verified. "Good people." "People with integrity." "Someone who cares."

**How it works:**
1. Accept the value. Do not dismiss it.
2. Ask for the observable signal that tells you this trait is present.
3. Lock in the signal, not the value.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "They need to be a good person."
>
> You: "I believe you, but 'good person' isn't something you can verify in a DM. What does a good person actually do in your experience? What's the signal you pick up on in the first conversation that tells you this person is solid?"
>
> Them: "I guess... they're grateful? They say thanks, they acknowledge the help, they don't just take and disappear."
>
> You: "So the behavioral signal is: shows gratitude, acknowledges value received, reciprocates effort. Someone who takes your free advice and ghosts is a red flag. That's verifiable."

---

## Technique 6: The Forced Choice

**When to use:** They are stuck between two options and cannot decide, or they keep saying "both" or "it depends."

**How it works:**
1. Remove the option to choose both.
2. Present a concrete scenario where they must pick.
3. Their pick reveals the real priority.

**Example sequence:**

> Them: "I want clients who are already making money but I also want to help total beginners."
>
> You: "Two people DM you today. Person A is making $500/month, has been posting for 6 months, has 800 followers, and is hungry. Person B is making $8k/month, has 15k followers, and wants to hit $20k. You can only take one. Who do you pick and why?"
>
> Them: "Person B. They've already proven they can do it."
>
> You: "Then 'total beginners' is your free content audience, not your ICP. Your paying client is Person B. Let's build the profile around that."

---

## Technique 7: The Past Evidence Check

**When to use:** They are describing an imaginary ideal client that may not match who actually buys from them.

**How it works:**
1. Ask who has actually paid them or come closest to paying.
2. Compare the real buyer to the described ideal.
3. If they diverge, flag it and ask which one they're building for.

**Example sequence:**

> You: "You've described your ideal client as a 25-year-old high-energy coach making $10k/month. Now tell me — who actually paid you in the last 6 months? What were they like?"
>
> Them: "Honestly, it was mostly people in their early 30s making like $3-5k."
>
> You: "So the person you described and the person who actually buys are different. That's important. Are you building the ICP around who you want, or who actually converts? Both are valid strategies, but they lead to different profiles."

---

## Technique 8: The Elimination Ladder

**When to use:** They truly cannot narrow down and every other technique has stalled. Last resort.

**How it works:**
1. Start absurdly broad.
2. Remove one category at a time.
3. Each removal forces them to react — their reaction tells you what matters.

**Example sequence:**

> You: "Let's try this differently. I'm going to start broad and we'll cut. Your client — do they run a business, or are they employed?"
>
> Them: "Run a business."
>
> You: "Product or service?"
>
> Them: "Service."
>
> You: "Do they sell to consumers or to other businesses?"
>
> Them: "Other businesses, mostly."
>
> You: "Are they solo or do they have a team?"
>
> Them: "Solo or maybe one VA."
>
> You: "Good — we've gone from 'anyone' to 'solo service provider selling B2B' in four questions. Now we can get specific."

## Handling Contradictions

Contradictions are not mistakes — they are the person holding two unresolved beliefs at once. Your job is to surface the conflict and make them choose.

### The Formula
1. State both things they said. Use their exact words.
2. Name why they conflict in concrete terms.
3. Ask them to pick one or explain how both can be true.
4. Do not proceed until resolved.

### Common Contradictions and How to Frame Them

**"I serve beginners" + revenue requirement:**
"You said you help beginners, but you also said they need to be making $5k/month. Someone making $5k/month isn't a beginner — they've already figured out the hard part. Which one is your actual client: the true beginner with no revenue, or the person who's got something working but needs to scale?"

**"I help everyone" + specific niche language:**
"You said you help anyone, but when you described your best client you talked about coaches and content creators specifically. Those are niches. Helping 'everyone' and writing content that speaks to coaches are different strategies. Which one are you actually doing?"

**"Price doesn't matter" + describing premium clients:**
"You said price isn't a filter, but everything you've described — the lifestyle, the investment mindset, the willingness to pay for coaching — that's a premium buyer. Price is filtering for you whether you've named it or not. What's the actual number?"

**Wide age range + specific lifestyle:**
"You said 16-45, but the lifestyle you described — gym daily, consuming Hormozi, chasing financial freedom, believes the corporate path is broken — that's a 24-30 year old. A 16-year-old might have the energy, but they're living a fundamentally different life. Who are you actually picturing when you write?"

## Signals That an Answer Is Too Thin

Watch for these. When you spot them, push back immediately.

- **One-word or one-sentence answers.** Always push. "That's too thin for me to work with. Give me the full picture."
- **Adjectives without examples.** "Driven," "motivated," "coachable," "good person." Translate to behavior.
- **"It depends."** Force a concrete scenario and make them choose.
- **"I don't know."** Flip to the mirror technique or the worst-client inversion.
- **"Anyone who..."** This is never true. Push for who specifically.
- **Listing 10+ items with no prioritization.** Force a top 3, or use the elimination ladder.
- **Aspirational language.** "I want to attract six-figure entrepreneurs" — have they ever worked with one? If not, this is fantasy, not ICP. Use the past evidence check.

---

## Tone When Pushing Back

Direct but not hostile. You are a skilled interviewer, not a drill sergeant. The energy is: "I need better information to do my job, and I'm going to help you get there."

Good: "That's too broad for me to build on. Let me come at it differently."
Good: "I hear you, but 'good person' isn't something I can put in a document. Give me the behavioral version."
Good: "You're describing two different people. Let's figure out which one is actually your client."

Bad: "That answer is wrong."
Bad: "You clearly haven't thought about this."
Bad: "That doesn't make any sense."
