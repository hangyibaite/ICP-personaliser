# Question Framework

The complete questioning sequence for the ICP interview. Each round has core questions, guiding reframes for when people are stuck, and notes on what to push on.

For handling vague or confused answers, see `references/ambiguity-handling.md`.
For synthesizing and confirming back, see `synthesis.md`.

---

## General Interview Principles

### Pacing
- 3-5 questions per round. Never more.
- Wait for answers before asking the next round.
- Use their exact words and phrases from prior answers in follow-up questions.

### Pushing for Depth
- Minimum useful answer: 100-300 words. Flag anything shorter.
- When pushing: "Talk me through this like you're explaining it to a friend — speak it out loud rather than typing if that's easier, most people get better answers that way."
- Never accept adjectives without behavioral definitions. "Driven" is meaningless. "They message back within 2 hours and have already tried three things before asking" is a real signal.

## Round 1: What They Actually Do

**Purpose:** One clear paragraph of what the business does and who benefits. Everything downstream depends on this.

**Core questions:**
- "In one paragraph, what does your business actually do? Not the mission statement — what do you literally deliver to a client and what changes for them after?"
- "If a friend asked 'so what do you do?' at a dinner party and you had 30 seconds, what would you say?"

**If they give jargon or buzzwords:**
- "Forget the marketing language. Tell me about the last client you worked with — what was their situation before, what did you do, and what happened after?"
- "If you had to explain this to your mother, who knows nothing about your industry, how would you say it?"

**Checkpoint: MANDATORY.** Synthesize into a tight paragraph. Confirm before proceeding.

---

## Round 2: The Priority Filter

**Purpose:** The single most important filter that sits above everything. If this is missing, nothing else matters.

**Core questions:**
- "How do your best clients actually find you? Not how you wish they found you — how do the ones who pay discover you exist? Walk me through the exact chain."
- "If you could only keep one channel, one method of getting clients, and had to delete everything else, which one survives?"
- "Is there a specific behavior or situation the client must be in for your service to even work? Something that, without it, there's nothing for you to grab onto?"

**Guiding reframe:**
"If someone came to you tomorrow — perfect in every other way, great person, right budget, right niche — but they were missing [this one thing], could you still help them? If the answer is no, that's your priority filter."

**Synthesis:** Frame as a single sentence: "Before anything else, they must [X]." Confirm.

---

## Round 3: Hard Prerequisites

**Purpose:** Binary pass/fail gates. Not preferences — hard requirements. If any one is missing, the person is not a fit right now.

**Core questions:**
- "Beyond the priority filter, what else must be true before you'd consider working with them? Pass/fail only."
- "What's the minimum viable starting point? Existing audience, existing offer, existing revenue, existing skills — where's the floor?"
- "Have you ever taken on a client who was missing something fundamental and it went badly? What were they missing?"
- "Is there a financial gate? Specific investment amount, or an earning threshold?"

**If they say "they just need to be motivated":**
- "Motivation isn't a gate — everyone thinks they're motivated. What does a motivated person actually have in place already? What have they done that proves they're serious, not just excited?"
- "Think about your worst client. What was missing that, if you'd known upfront, you would've said no? That missing thing is a prerequisite."

**Push for verifiability:** Every prerequisite must be observable. For each one, ask: "How would you verify this in a 5-minute conversation?"

**Synthesis:** Numbered binary statements. Confirm: "If any one of these is missing, they're not a fit right now. Agreed?"

---

## Round 4: What They Sell & How

**Purpose:** Map the offer structure and sales mechanism.

**Core questions:**
- "Walk me through exactly what someone buys from you. Core offer, and any other tiers or levels."
- "How does the sale happen? DMs? Sales call? Checkout page? Webinar? What's the last step before they pay?"
- "What platform(s) does this live on? Where does the client journey start and end?"

**Multiple offers:** "Which one do you most want to fill? That's the one we build the ICP around."

**Synthesis:** Brief summary: what they sell, delivery method, sales mechanism, platforms. Confirm.

---

## Round 5: The Money

**Purpose:** Financial parameters — what they charge, what their clients can afford, unit economics.

**Core questions:**
- "What do you charge, or plan to charge? Actual number, not a range."
- "What does your client typically earn? Can they afford your price without it being a crisis?"
- "Is earning capacity a filter, or have you dropped it?"

**If uncomfortable talking money:**
"I'm not asking you to justify your price. I need the number because it determines what kind of person can actually buy from you. A $200 product and a $10,000 service attract fundamentally different humans."

**If B2B:** "What's the lifetime value of their clients to them? Different number from your price — I need both."

**Synthesis:** Price point(s), financial gates, client LTV if applicable. Confirm.

---

## Round 6: The Person

**Purpose:** Full psychographic, demographic, and behavioral profile. One specific human, not a category. This is the longest section. Split into sub-rounds.

### 6a: Demographics & Life Situation
- "What's the age of your typical client? Not a range — the median. If your range is wider than 10 years, I'm going to push back."
- "Gender split — does it skew or even?"
- "Geography or language requirements?"
- "Life situation: single grinding, married with kids, student, day-job escapee? Be specific."
- "What does their Tuesday at 11pm look like? Literally — what are they doing, thinking, feeling?"

**If wide age range:** "You said 18-40. An 18-year-old and a 40-year-old are different people. When you write content, who are you actually picturing? Close your eyes. One person is reading your post. How old are they? That's your avatar."

### 6b: Personality & Values
- "Describe the personality of your favorite client interaction. What made it click? What did they say or do?"
- "Describe the worst. What personality trait made you want to end it?"
- "What do they believe about money? Investing in themselves obvious, or agonize over every dollar?"
- "Conventional thinkers or contrarian? Trust institutions or distrust them?"
- "What opinions do they hold that most in the space would disagree with?"

### 6c: Media Diet & Cultural Identity
- "What online figures do they follow? Not just your niche — who do they look up to broadly?"
- "If they have a bookshelf, what's actually on it?"
- "What do they do at 9pm on a Saturday not thinking about business?"

### 6d: The Delayed Flight Test
- "Stuck on a 3-hour delayed flight next to your ideal client. What do you talk about? Not business — what else?"

If they still can't answer after all fallbacks, say directly: "The fact that you can't picture this conversation means we haven't narrowed the avatar enough. Let's keep going."

**Synthesis:** Full portrait paragraph describing one specific human. Read back and confirm: "Does this sound like one real person you could picture?"

---

## Round 7: Hard Vibe Deal-Breakers

**Core questions:**
- "Who do you refuse to work with even if they tick every box and have unlimited budget?"
- "Have you ever fired a client or wished you had? What were they like?"
- "What behavior drains your energy fastest in a working relationship?"

**Push for specificity:** Translate vague answers into behavioral signals. Not "lazy" — "treats the engagement as a purchase, expects results delivered without doing work, responds in 3-4 business days, never implements anything between sessions."

**Synthesis:** Bulleted list of specific, observable behaviors.

---

## Round 8: Adaptive Section(s)

Not a fixed round. Throughout the interview, track recurring emphasis. Create a section only if:
- They mentioned it more than twice unprompted
- They corrected you when you downplayed it
- They said "this is really important" or equivalent
- It doesn't fit into existing sections but matters for who they work with

Common adaptive sections: Content & Engagement Signals, Technical Skill Floor, Industry-Specific Qualifiers, Team & Infrastructure, Mindset & Coachability Markers.

If nothing emerged, skip entirely. Do not force it.

---

## Round 9: Niches

**Core questions:**
- "List every type of person or business you'd happily serve. Broad categories."
- "Now list every type that's a hard no — will not touch even with cash in hand. What and why?"

**Too narrow (3 or fewer):** "Are there adjacent categories sharing the same core problem? Who else has the same pain in a different wrapper?"

**Too broad (15+):** "That's a Yellow Pages entry, not a niche list. Which have you actually served? Which are guesses? Cut the guesses."

**Synthesis:** Two lists: niches served, hard no. Confirm both.

---

## Round 10: Quick Reference

Generate from confirmed sections. No new questions. Two bullet lists: Strong Fit, Not a Fit.

Read back for final confirmation.
